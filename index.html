Comets:

Comets are small objects that travel through space and orbit the Sun. They are often called "dirty snowballs" because they are made of ice mixed with dust and rocky material. When a comet gets close to the Sun, the heat causes its ice to turn into gas, creating a glowing cloud called a coma and a long, bright tail. This tail always points away from the Sun because it is pushed by solar wind and sunlight.

Comets formed about 4.6 billion years ago when the Solar System was first created. They are made mostly of frozen water, carbon dioxide, methane, ammonia, dust, and rock. Most comets come from two distant regions of the Solar System called the Kuiper Belt and the Oort Cloud. Scientists study comets because they contain material that has remained mostly unchanged since the Solar System formed, helping us learn about its history.

Some well-known examples of comets include Halley's Comet, Comet Hale-Bopp, and Comet NEOWISE. Halley's Comet is the most famous because it can be seen from Earth about every 76 years. Comet Hale-Bopp was one of the brightest comets of the 20th century and was visible for many months in 1997.
